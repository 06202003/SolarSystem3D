Jupiter, the largest planet in our solar system, boasts a remarkable collection of over 79 known satellites. Among these, the Galilean Moons—Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto—stand out as the most significant and were first observed by Galileo Galilei in 1610. Over the years, space exploration has sent 39 missions to study Jupiter, starting with Pioneer 10 in 1973 and culminating in the Juno spacecraft's visit in 2016.
Ganymede, Jupiter's largest moon, is noteworthy for emitting nonthermal narrowband radio emissions. During mutual occultations and eclipses, the satellites exhibit reduced brightness, a phenomenon observed as they pass in close proximity to each other.

Io, another of Jupiter's intriguing moons, showcases active volcanic activity, with sulfur plumes adding to its dynamic character.
